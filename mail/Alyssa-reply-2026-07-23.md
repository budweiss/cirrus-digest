Subject: Teaching vocabulary the Science of Reading way - research + strategies

Hi Alyssa,

Here's the workup you asked for: research-backed, Science of Reading
aligned ways to teach vocabulary, with the sources at the bottom. I've
framed the classroom examples for your 4th graders. The through-line
across the research is that vocabulary is one of the five pillars of
skilled reading (National Reading Panel, 2000) and that it's taught best
through rich, active, repeated engagement with words - not memorizing
lists and definitions.

Ten ways to teach vocabulary effectively:

  1. Teach a small set of high-utility "Tier 2" words directly.
     Beck, McKeown & Kucan sort words into three tiers. Tier 2 words are
     the high-payoff ones: they show up across many texts and subjects
     but rarely in everyday talk (e.g. reluctant, analyze, contrast,
     summarize). Pull 5-8 from a read-aloud or unit text each week and
     teach them on purpose. Skip Tier 1 (already known) and be selective
     with Tier 3 (technical, teach as needed in content).

  2. Give student-friendly explanations, not dictionary definitions.
     Explain a word in plain, everyday language, with an example and a
     non-example ("reluctant means you don't really want to do something
     - like being reluctant to get out of a warm bed on a cold morning").
     Dictionary definitions are often more confusing than the word.

  3. Build in multiple, varied encounters over time.
     One exposure isn't enough. Students need to hear, say, read, AND
     write a word across several days and contexts before it sticks.
     Space the practice out and keep bringing words back rather than
     "teaching and moving on."

  4. Make students USE the words actively.
     "Robust" instruction has kids do something with the word - answer a
     question with it, decide if a situation is an example of it, finish
     a sentence stem, act it out, or talk with a partner. Active use
     beats passive re-reading of definitions every time.

  5. Teach morphology - roots, prefixes, and suffixes.
     Showing students how words are built lets them unlock words they've
     never seen. Fourth grade is a great point for common affixes (re-,
     un-, -tion, -able, -ly) and Greek/Latin roots (port = carry: import,
     transport, portable). Start from a word they know, then grow the
     family.

  6. Pre-teach key words before reading.
     Front-loading a handful of words that are essential to a text
     measurably improves comprehension of that text. Choose the few words
     a student truly needs to follow the passage, not every hard word.

  7. Teach word-learning strategies for independence.
     Kids meet far more words than we can ever pre-teach, so teach them
     HOW to figure words out: use context clues, break the word into
     parts, and cross-check that the guess makes sense in the sentence.
     The goal is students who can learn words on their own.

  8. Map word relationships (semantic maps, Frayer model).
     Deepen word knowledge by connecting words to each other - categorize
     them, compare/contrast, sort synonyms and antonyms, or use a Frayer
     model (definition, characteristics, examples, non-examples). This
     builds depth, not just a surface label.

  9. Read aloud rich texts and TALK about the words.
     Interactive read-alouds of books above students' own reading level
     expose them to sophisticated vocabulary in context, and the
     discussion around them is where a lot of word learning happens.
     Pause to notice and use juicy words as they come up.

 10. Grow "word consciousness" and wide reading.
     Help students notice, play with, and enjoy words - wordplay, "word
     of the day," celebrating when someone uses a target word in the
     wild. Paired with lots of independent reading, this is what builds
     vocabulary breadth over the long haul.

A practical weekly rhythm that pulls these together: pick 5-8 Tier 2
words from your read-aloud (1), give kid-friendly explanations (2),
revisit them daily with quick active-use tasks (3, 4), tie in a
morphology or word-relationship mini-lesson (5, 8), and keep a running
word wall you actually refer back to (3, 10).
